Game Theory

Strategic decision-making when outcomes depend on the actions of multiple players

Overview

Game theory analyzes strategic interactions where each player's optimal choice depends on what they expect others to do. It's fundamental to understanding competition, cooperation, and strategic behavior in economics.

Key Concepts

Players, Strategies, and Payoffs

Players: Decision-makers in the game
Strategies: Complete plans of action for each player
Payoffs: Outcomes or utilities each player receives

Nash Equilibrium

A set of strategies where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy. Each player's strategy is a best response to others' strategies.

Dominant Strategy

A strategy that yields a higher payoff than any other strategy, regardless of what other players do.

Dominated Strategy

A strategy that always yields a lower payoff than some other strategy. Rational players never play dominated strategies.

Classic Example: Prisoner's Dilemma

Two suspects are interrogated separately. Each can confess or stay silent.

Payoff Matrix (years in prison)
Suspect 2: Silent Suspect 2: Confess
Suspect 1: Silent -1, -1 -10, 0
Suspect 1: Confess 0, -10 -5, -5

Nash Equilibrium: Both confess (-5, -5)
Key Insight: Individual rationality leads to worse collective outcome than mutual cooperation (-1, -1)

Types of Games

  • Simultaneous vs. Sequential: Players move at the same time or in turns
  • Zero-Sum vs. Non-Zero-Sum: One player's gain equals another's loss, or mutual gains/losses possible
  • Cooperative vs. Non-Cooperative: Whether binding agreements are possible
  • Perfect vs. Imperfect Information: Whether players know all previous moves
  • Repeated Games: Same game played multiple times (enables cooperation through reputation)

Solution Concepts

Backward Induction

Solving sequential games by working backwards from the end. Used to find subgame perfect equilibria.

Mixed Strategy Equilibrium

When players randomize between strategies with specific probabilities. Each player is indifferent between their pure strategies.

Practice Problems

  1. Find all Nash equilibria in a 2x2 game with given payoff matrix.
  2. Determine if any players have dominant strategies.
  3. Solve a sequential game using backward induction.
  4. Calculate the mixed strategy Nash equilibrium for matching pennies.
  5. Analyze how cooperation can emerge in repeated prisoner's dilemma.
